The Preservation of Nepalese Folk Rhythms in Global Metropolises

As diaspora communities grow across Western cities, the ancient folk rhythms of Nepal face both unprecedented opportunity and quiet erosion.

Read Article

The story of Nepalese folk dance in New York is, at its core, a story about memory. Every dhamaka beat that resonates through a Queens studio carries the dust of Terai fields, the mountain air of Dharan, and the festival energy of Indra Jatra.

For over three decades, Gobind Rai has been a custodian of these rhythms — not in a museum, but in a living classroom. His students range from Nepali-American children who have never visited their parents' homeland to professional dancers seeking to expand their physical vocabulary.

The challenge is authenticity without rigidity. Folk dance was never static; it evolved with communities, seasons, and celebrations. Teaching it in Elmhurst means honoring its roots while acknowledging that the soil has changed. The feet are the same. The floor is different.

What matters is the intention — the rasa — behind every movement. That, Rai insists, is universal.

The Choreographic Eye: Camera Directing Tips for Modern Dancers

The stage and the screen demand fundamentally different physical vocabularies. A masterclass in understanding the invisible rectangle.

On stage, a dancer performs for the last row. On camera, they perform for a lens six feet away. This distinction — seemingly simple — transforms everything: the scale of gestures, the architecture of formations, the timing of transitions.

Gobind Rai learned this through decades of navigating Kollywood film sets, where a single take might cost more than a week of stage rehearsal. The camera doesn't forgive imprecision the way a live audience does. It amplifies everything.

From Dharan Roots to Elmhurst Avenues: A 30-Year Cross-Continental Journey

Tracing the arc of a life lived between two worlds — from the foothill stages of eastern Nepal to the corridors of Elmhurst, Queens.

Dharan in the early 1970s was a garrison town in transition. British Gurkha recruitment was winding down, and the city was finding a new identity. Into this shifting landscape, Gobind Rai was born — not to a family of performers, but to one that understood discipline.

The dance came later, almost by accident. A school cultural program. A teacher who noticed something in the way the boy moved. A local festival that needed young performers. These small moments accumulated into a calling.

By the mid-1990s, Rai was in Kathmandu, founding The Angel Kalakendra and reshaping what dance training looked like in Nepal. His students didn't just learn steps — they learned presence, camera awareness, and the business of performance.

Why Every Dancer Should Study Classical Foundations

Before innovation comes understanding. The argument for building your movement vocabulary from the ground up.

In an era where viral choreography can be learned in an afternoon, the case for spending months — even years — on classical foundations may seem counterintuitive. But ask any choreographer who has worked across genres, and they will tell you the same thing: classical training is not a style. It is a language.

The mudras of Bharatanatyam, the footwork patterns of Kathak, the postural discipline of Nepali folk — these are not relics of a past era. They are technologies of the body, refined over centuries, that give dancers access to a range of expression that contemporary training alone cannot provide.
